The CorpX security team flagged a machine on the internal network. During a 20-second window, 192.168.1.78 sent repeated DNS queries to an unknown external domain — every query came back NXDOMAIN.

Someone may have used DNS tunneling to smuggle data out. A packet capture was taken at the egress point. Open it in Wireshark, trace the suspicious traffic, and reconstruct what was exfiltrated.

Objectives
  • 1
    Download and open the PCAP file
  • 2
    Isolate DNS traffic and find the suspicious source IP
  • 3
    Collect the 4 subdomain labels in timestamp order
  • 4
    Decode the labels and reconstruct the flag

Open with Wireshark, tshark, or tcpdump. Free download at wireshark.org for Windows, Linux, macOS.
Hints
-10 pts
Filter by dns and look at the Source IP column. One machine on the 192.168.1.x subnet is making all 4 suspicious queries — it is not the same machine doing normal internal DNS lookups.
-15 pts
The parent domain is data.corpx-sync.net. Each of the 4 queries uses a different subdomain prefix. Each prefix is a chunk of encoded data. Collect them in timestamp order.
-25 pts
The labels are Base32 encoded (lowercase, no padding). Join the 4 labels into one string, convert to uppercase, pad with = until the length is a multiple of 8, then Base32 decode. CyberChef works well for this.

DNS tunneling routes data through subdomain labels, bypassing firewalls that allow DNS but block HTTP. Defenders should alert on repeated NXDOMAIN responses to the same unknown parent domain from a single internal host.
